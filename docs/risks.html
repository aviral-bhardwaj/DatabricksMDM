G · Critical Risks, Trade-offs & Differentiators

Be honest about what you give up — and ruthless about what you uniquely gain

Building native means out-engineering thirty years of incumbent investment in some areas. You will not win all of them. The discipline is knowing which battles to concede (buy/borrow) and which constitute a moat no closed hub can cross.

Why stewardship UI staying outside is correct (not a cop-out)

The single most common objection: "if it's native, why isn't the UI in Databricks?" Because forcing an interactive, high-concurrency, transactional UX onto a data-and-compute plane produces a worse product on every axis:

  • Workload mismatch. Stewardship is thousands of small reads/writes with optimistic locking and rich form state — an OLTP pattern. Spark is an analytical engine; abusing it for per-click transactions is slow and expensive.
  • Latency & concurrency. Stewards expect sub-second interactions; cluster spin-up and job semantics don't fit.
  • Separation of concerns. The state (cases, decisions, assignments) is data and belongs in Delta — fully native, fully audited. The rendering of that state is a presentation concern that belongs in a web app.
The boundary that keeps it native

The workbench writes decisions back to mdm_steward Delta tables; those decisions flow into mastering via CDF; the audit trail is the data. Nothing about identity, truth, or governance leaves the lakehouse. Only pixels do. That is the correct seam — and it's exactly how the strongest SaaS vendors separate their UI tier from their data tier anyway. (A Databricks App can host it for convenience without changing the principle.)

Whitespace: where a Databricks-native product is superior

Openness

Master in open Delta, logic in open code, no proprietary store. Zero lock-in to a mastering vendor; your golden record outlives any tool choice.

Proximity to data

The master is queryable in-place by every BI tool, notebook, model, and Genie space — no export, no API hop, no staleness. This is structurally impossible for a Tier-2 SaaS hub.

AI integration

Vector Search + served FMs + MLflow let you do semantic match and LLM adjudication against your data without exfiltrating it. A closed hub must ship your PII to its models or yours to it.

Governance lineage

UC lineage spans source → master → consumption in one graph. Incumbents can only show lineage inside their hub; the lakehouse shows it end-to-end.

Cost model

DBUs + storage, elastic and serverless-capable — versus per-record/per-domain/per-seat licensing that punishes growth.

Extensibility

Strategy contracts mean a new match algorithm or domain is a plugin + YAML, not a vendor feature request on a roadmap you don't control.

Data-product alignment

The golden record is a governed data product with owners, contracts, SLAs, and discovery — not a sync target. It fits mesh/marketplace directly.

Reprocessing & trust experiments

Time travel lets you replay a new survivorship rule against full history and A/B trust models — a capability hubs with mutable stores simply cannot offer.

The strategic line

Concede stewardship polish, packaged domains, and the long connector tail — borrow or phase them. Own openness, data proximity, AI-in-place, end-to-end lineage, cost, and reprocessing. Those six are not features an incumbent can ship; they are consequences of architecture. That asymmetry is the entire reason to build native instead of buying Reltio.
